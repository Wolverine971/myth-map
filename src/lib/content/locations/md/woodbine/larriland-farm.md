## Overview

Larriland Farm is a 285-acre, family-owned pick-your-own farm in western Howard County, run by the Moore family since 1973. You walk the fields, fill a container with whatever is ripe that week, and carry it out — strawberries in late May, berries and peaches through summer, apples and pumpkins in fall. It's a working farm with over 20 crops, not a petting-zoo-style attraction, so the fun is the picking itself plus a fall festival with hayrides and mazes.

A $3-per-person field ticket is required to enter the pick-your-own fields for anyone 3 and up (kids 2 and under free), on top of paying for the fruit you pick. The Red Barn farm market is free to browse with no ticket. This suits families who want a few hours outside and don't mind gravel, grass, and dust. Skip it if you need flat paved paths, real flush bathrooms, or a quick rain-proof stop — it's all outdoors and weather-dependent.

## What to know before you go

- **Season:** Opens late May or early June (whenever strawberries ripen) and runs through mid-to-late November. Closed all winter.
- **Hours (opening day–Oct 31):** Closed Mondays. Tuesday–Friday 9am–6pm. Saturday & Sunday 9am–5pm.
- **Holiday hours:** Memorial Day, Juneteenth, July 4, Labor Day, Yom Kippur, and Columbus Day run a short 9am–1pm.
- **November hours:** First three weeks, Thursday–Sunday 10:30am–4:30pm only.
- **Pick-your-own ticket:** A $3-per-person field ticket is required to enter the fields, everyone 3 and older (toddlers 2 and under free). One ticket covers every open field that day — you get a wristband at the entrance. A season pass (unlimited field access plus 5% off picked produce) is worth it for regulars. Fall festival weekends (Sat & Sun) run a higher $14 per person that bundles in hayrides and mazes. No ticket needed for the farm market.
- **Crop pricing:** Charged by the container, not by the pound. You buy a container at the field entrance, and the container size sets how many people can go into the field.
- **Containers:** Only Larriland containers are allowed in the fields — you can't bring your own.
- **Payment:** Cash, credit cards, and tap-to-pay.
- **Bags:** Leave large purses and bags in the car; large bags, wagons, and strollers may be inspected.
- **Restrooms:** Port-a-potties only — no flush restrooms, no changing tables, no nursing room.
- **Parking:** Free gravel and grass lots by the Big Red Barn and out at the fields.
- **What to bring:** Sunscreen, hats, and water (water is sold on-site, reusable bottles encouraged), plus closed-toe shoes for muddy rows.

## Tips for families

- **Call the hotline or check Today's Harvest before you leave home.** What's ripe changes day to day with weather and demand — 410-442-2605 is a recorded line, and the [Today's Harvest page](https://www.pickyourown.com/todays-harvest.html) lists exactly which fields are open.
- **Go on a weekday if you can.** Tuesday–Friday hours run later (until 6pm) and crowds are thin; fall festival weekends are the busiest and priciest stretch at $14 a head versus the standard $3 field ticket.
- **Buy your field ticket online before fall weekends** to skip the entry line during pumpkin season.
- **Skip the stroller in the fields.** Rows are gravel, grass, and often muddy. A baby carrier works far better, and strollers can be held up for inspection at the entrance anyway.
- **Bring your own bucket for the kids to carry** — but remember the actual picking container must be a Larriland one bought at the field.
- **Plan for port-a-potties.** Take little ones before you head deep into the orchard, and pack wipes since there are no changing tables.
- **Match the crop to the kid.** Strawberries and pumpkins sit low and are easy for toddlers; peaches and apples on trees suit older kids who can reach.
- **Save the hayride, hay maze, and Boo Barn for fall festival weekends** (roughly September through October), when those activities run.

## Best time to visit

Strawberries open the season in late May and early June — the easiest crop for little hands. Blueberries and other berries run late June into summer, peaches arrive mid-to-late summer, and apples start in early September. The fall festival, with pumpkins, hayrides, mazes, and the Boo Barn, fills September and October and is the marquee family stretch.

For the calmest visit, come on a weekday morning right at 9am opening. Fall Saturdays and Sundays are the most crowded, so arrive early for parking and shorter ticket lines. Everything is outdoors, so a steady rain or a 95-degree afternoon makes for a rough trip — there's little shade and no indoor backup beyond the market barn.

## FAQs

**Do I need a ticket to pick?**
Yes. A $3-per-person field ticket is required for everyone 3 and older to enter the pick-your-own fields (kids 2 and under free); one ticket covers all open fields that day. Fall festival weekends cost more ($14 per person). You still pay separately for the fruit you pick, and the Red Barn farm market is free with no ticket.

**How does the pricing work?**
Crops are charged by the container, not by weight. You buy a Larriland container at the field entrance, and its size determines how many people can enter the field with it.

**Is there parking?**
Yes — free gravel and grass lots by the Big Red Barn and out at the fields. It can get tight on fall weekends, so come early.

**Are there bathrooms?**
Only port-a-potties. There are no flush restrooms, changing tables, or a nursing room, so plan accordingly with babies and toddlers.

**Is it stroller-friendly?**
Not really. The fields are gravel, grass, and often muddy, and strollers may be inspected at the entrance. A baby carrier is the better bet for under-2s.

**Can I bring my dog?**
No. Pets aren't allowed; service dogs are the only exception.

**How long should we plan for?**
Most families spend 1.5 to 3 hours — enough to pick a crop or two, browse the market, and (in fall) do a hayride and maze.

**What if it rains?**
It's a fully outdoor visit, so steady rain usually means waiting for another day. Check the Today's Harvest hotline, since fields can close in bad weather.

**Are you open in winter?**
No. The farm closes from late November until strawberries ripen again in late May or early June. The farm market reopens with the season.
