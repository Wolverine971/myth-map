## Overview

The North Tract is the wilder half of Patuxent Research Refuge — meadows, forest, and a string of ponds on former Fort Meade military training land, connected by a paved loop road and gravel trails. It works like a do-it-yourself safari: you drive between stops, and kids count deer, turkeys, and herons from the back seat or from short walks at each pull-off. Admission is free. The catch is big, though: [the North Tract is open Sundays only, 8 a.m. to 4 p.m.](https://www.fws.gov/refuge/patuxent-research/visit-us), a staffing-driven schedule in place since late 2023.

This is a spot for families who pack binoculars, bikes, or a fishing pole and make their own fun. There is no playground, no exhibit hall, and nothing for sale — not even snacks. If your kids need more structure, or Sunday doesn't work, the refuge's South Tract and its [National Wildlife Visitor Center](https://www.fws.gov/refuge/patuxent-research/patuxent-visitor-center) (open Wednesday–Saturday) is the better pick.

## What to know before you go

- **Hours:** Sundays only, 8 a.m.–4 p.m. Closed federal holidays and occasionally for scheduled hunts — check the [refuge site](https://www.fws.gov/refuge/patuxent-research/visit-us) or call (301) 497-5770 before driving out.
- **Cost:** Free. No entrance fee, no parking fee.
- **Sign-in is mandatory.** Everyone checks in and out at the Visitor Contact Station, about a mile down Bald Eagle Drive from the Route 198 entrance. Budget a few extra minutes on each end.
- **Parking:** Free lots at the Visitor Contact Station, the Wildlife Viewing Area, and Lake Allen, so you're never far from the car.
- **Bathrooms:** At the Visitor Contact Station and the Wildlife Viewing Area, both wheelchair-accessible. Nothing else out on the trails.
- **Food:** Nothing is sold on the refuge. Bring your own water and snacks.
- **Strollers:** The paved road and gravel roads roll fine with a jogging stroller. The natural-surface foot trails do not — bring a carrier for babies and toddlers.
- **Dogs:** Allowed on a leash 6 feet or shorter, held in hand. Pack out the waste.
- **Safety rule that isn't optional:** This is former Army training land with a real unexploded-ordnance risk and active gun ranges nearby. Everyone stays on roads and marked trails, full stop.
- **What to bring:** Binoculars, bug spray, long pants, closed shoes, water, and snacks.

## Tips for families

- Treat it as a drive-and-stop loop, not a hike. The [Wildlife Loop](https://birdersguidemddc.org/site/patuxent-research-refuge-north-tract/) is roughly eight miles of paved road; the usual family circuit is Contact Station → Wildlife Viewing Area at Merganser Pond → Lake Allen, with short walks at each.
- Arrive at the 8 a.m. opening. Deer, turkeys, and waterbirds are out in the first couple of hours and thin out by midday.
- Have the stay-on-the-trail talk before anyone gets out of the car. "There's old Army explosives in the woods" is the rare safety rule kids follow without arguing.
- Ticks are the local hazard. Long pants tucked into socks, spray before you walk, full-body check when you get home.
- Start at the Visitor Contact Station feeders and pollinator garden — hummingbirds in summer, songbirds year-round. It's the one guaranteed wildlife sighting for a toddler with a 90-second attention span.
- Grab the paper trail map at sign-in and ask the staff what's closed. Trail openings change with hunt seasons, and the website doesn't always keep up.
- Gravel roads suit confident bike riders; some stretches have loose, golf-ball-sized rock. Skip the training-wheels crew.
- Kids under 16 [fish free without a license](https://www.fws.gov/refuge/patuxent-research/visit-us/activities/fishing), but only in designated areas, and Lake Allen is open on selected dates only — call (301) 497-5770 first. Earthworms are the only live bait allowed, and lead sinkers are banned.
- Watch the clock. The gate closes at 4 p.m. and you have to sign out at the Contact Station on the way — start heading back by 3:30.

## Best time to visit

- **Day of week:** Sunday is the only option. Plan around it.
- **Time of day:** The 8 a.m. opening is prime time for wildlife. Late morning is the quietest stretch for a picnic-blanket break; animal activity picks back up before the 4 p.m. close, but you'll be racing the sign-out.
- **Season:** Spring and fall are the sweet spot — bird migration, mild temps, fewer bugs. Summer works if you're done walking by noon; heat and ticks own the afternoon, but the hummingbird feeders at the Contact Station are at their busiest. Winter is the quietest and works fine as a heated-car wildlife drive with short walks.
- **Weather contingency:** There is none — everything is outdoors. In steady rain, go to the South Tract's indoor visitor center (Wednesday–Saturday) instead, or reschedule.

## FAQs

**Is the North Tract really only open one day a week?**
Yes. Since October 2023 it has been open to general visitors on Sundays only, 8 a.m. to 4 p.m., because of reduced refuge staffing. It also closes on federal holidays and for occasional scheduled hunts, so check the refuge website or call (301) 497-5770 before you go.

**Does it cost anything?**
No. Entry and parking are free. The only cost is a Maryland non-tidal fishing license if anyone 16 or older wants to fish.

**Can we drive it, or do we have to hike?**
You can do the whole visit as a drive. Paved road connects the Contact Station, the Wildlife Viewing Area, and Lake Allen, and plenty of wildlife is visible from the car. Walks are add-ons, not requirements.

**Is it stroller-friendly?**
Partly. Jogging strollers handle the paved and gravel roads fine, but the natural-surface foot trails are not stroller-passable. A carrier is the better call for under-2s if you plan to walk the trails.

**Are there bathrooms?**
Yes, at two spots: the Visitor Contact Station and the Wildlife Viewing Area. Both are wheelchair-accessible. There's no food or drink for sale anywhere on the tract, so pack in what you need.

**Can my kids fish there?**
Yes — kids under 16 don't need a license. Fishing is limited to designated areas during open hours, and Lake Allen, the most popular spot, is only open on selected dates. Call ahead, bring earthworms (the only live bait allowed), and leave lead sinkers at home.

**Are dogs allowed?**
Yes, on a leash 6 feet or shorter that stays in your hand. Clean up after them.

**Why is everyone so serious about staying on the trails?**
The land was an Army training and munitions area for decades, and unexploded ordnance is still a real possibility off-trail. Active gun ranges also border the refuge. Roads and marked trails are safe; the woods beyond them are genuinely off-limits.
