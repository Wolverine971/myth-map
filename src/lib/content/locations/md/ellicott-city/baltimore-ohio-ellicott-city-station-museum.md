## Overview

A small, free, two-floor stone station at the bottom of Main Street that happens to be [the oldest surviving railroad station in the United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellicott_City_station). Built in 1830 by the [Baltimore & Ohio Railroad](https://savingplaces.org/places/bo-ellicott-city-station-museum) as the original western terminus of the first 13 miles of commercial railroad track in America — and the spot where the Tom Thumb steam locomotive ran its famous race against a horse later that same year. [Operated by Howard County Recreation & Parks](https://www.howardcountymd.gov/ecborail) since 2017.

The kid draw is the stuff outside the main building: a **restored 1927 caboose** kids can climb into, and a **40-foot HO-scale model layout** in the 1885 Freight House that traces the original 13-mile route from Baltimore. Sweet spot is **roughly ages 4–9**, especially if there's a train obsession in the house. Skip it if your kids are pre-walking (the second floor is stairs-only) or older than about 11 — most exhibits are static interpretive panels and there's no hands-on activity past the caboose.

## What to know before you go

- **Hours:** Wednesday–Thursday 10 a.m.–3 p.m., Friday–Sunday 10 a.m.–5 p.m. **Closed Monday and Tuesday.** Also closed New Year's Day, Easter Sunday, Memorial Day, Juneteenth, July 4, Labor Day, Thanksgiving, and Christmas Day. Confirm on the [official site](https://www.howardcountymd.gov/ecborail) before driving.
- **Cost:** **Free admission.** Donations welcome at the front desk.
- **Guided tours:** $10 per person, by appointment only. Worth it for school-age kids if you can swing a weekday — the docents know the granular operational history. Contact Conor Conneally at 410-313-2924 or cconneally@howardcountymd.gov.
- **Parking:** A small free lot is right next to the museum off Maryland Avenue, plus on-street spots. If the lot is full, the [free public lots](https://visitellicottcity.com/getting-here/) in the historic district (Lots A, C, D, E, F) are walkable — Lot D behind the Welcome Center is the closest larger option.
- **Restrooms:** On the first floor inside the station building. Small and historic — assume no changing table; pack the changing pad if you're traveling with a baby.
- **Stroller-friendliness:** First-floor and the outdoor freight house and caboose are stroller-passable. **The second floor is stairs-only — no elevator.** A wheelchair ramp at the back of the building (the old Turntable Pit) gets non-stair users to the second floor; ask staff to point you to it.
- **Accessibility:** Partial. The exterior ramp gets you to the upper level; getting in and out of the 1927 caboose involves steep metal steps with handrails.
- **Active rail tracks:** The CSX line still runs along the back of the station. Trains pass through unannounced — exciting for train kids, jarring for sensory-sensitive ones. There's no schedule posted; it's luck.
- **Food:** None on site, no vending. Main Street is a 5-minute walk for cafes and ice cream.
- **Time investment:** **45 minutes to 90 minutes** is typical. Train-obsessed kids stretch it past two hours by camping at the HO layout; tweens are usually done in 30.
- **What to bring:** A small cash donation if you can, plus quarters if you want anything from the gift shop (Thomas merchandise, books, patches, lapel pins).

## Tips for families

- **Save the caboose for last.** Once kids climb in, they don't want to come out — and you still have a station and a freight house to walk through. Doing the indoor exhibits first means they'll actually look at them.
- **Hunt for the toy dinosaur and mermaid hidden in the HO model.** Volunteers tucked them into the layout as a long-running scavenger gag. Telling kids about them turns "watching trains" into "find the dino" and adds 20 minutes.
- **Go on a Wednesday or Thursday morning.** The museum is quietest on those weekday daytime hours; weekends draw the train-club regulars who'll happily explain the layout to your kid but also pack the freight house.
- **Pair it with the Ellicott Mills Children's Museum** four blocks up the hill if you have a half day to fill. The walk between them is the steepest part of Main Street — strap babies in carriers, not strollers, for the climb back down.
- **Ask about the scavenger hunt at the front desk.** It's not advertised on the website but the staff hand it out on request. Works well for ages 6–10.
- **Warn kids about the trains in advance.** The CSX line behind the building is active and the horns are loud. Train-loving kids will lose their minds (positive); noise-averse kids will need a heads-up.
- **Bring a small donation.** It's a free county museum running on tight funding — the donation jar is at the front desk and a few singles goes further than a credit-card swipe at a paid attraction.
- **The Holiday Train Garden is the best version of this place.** It runs late November through early January each year, free, and adds extra running layouts inside the station. If you only visit once, visit then — but expect parking to be a problem.
- **Skip if it's pouring.** The freight house and caboose are outdoors and you'll be walking between buildings. Light rain is fine; steady rain limits you to the small main building.

## Best time to visit

- **Time of day:** Friday or Saturday at 10 a.m. open is the lightest weekend window; weekday afternoons are the calmest overall. Last admission is effectively the closing time — there's no formal cutoff but staff start closing the freight house 15 minutes before closing.
- **Day of week:** Closed Mondays and Tuesdays. Wednesday–Thursday days are the quietest. Saturdays and Sundays are family-heavy from late morning on.
- **Season:** Year-round, but the **Holiday Train Garden (late November through early January)** is the standout — extra layouts, more activity, more staff out talking to kids. Spring and fall weekends are good for combining it with Main Street walking. Summer is fine but the freight house gets warm on hot afternoons.
- **Weather contingency:** It's a fine rainy-day hour-killer but most of the headline content is in the outdoor freight house and the outdoor caboose, so a steady downpour shrinks the visit to the small main building. Pair it with the indoor [Ellicott Mills Children's Museum](https://www.hchsmd.org/childrens-museum) if you're rain-trapped on Main Street.

## FAQs

**Is there parking?**
Yes — a free lot next to the museum off Maryland Avenue, plus the free [public historic-district lots](https://visitellicottcity.com/getting-here/) (A, C, D, E, F) within walking distance. Lot B is closed during the ongoing flood-mitigation construction.

**How long should we plan for?**
45 to 90 minutes is typical. Train-obsessed kids easily stretch it past two hours; older tweens often clear it in 30.

**What ages is this best for?**
Roughly 4–9 is the sweet spot — old enough to climb the caboose steps and watch the model trains, young enough to find static historical exhibits interesting. Skip it if your kid is pre-walking or already deep in middle school.

**Is it really free?**
Yes — free general admission. Guided tours, if you book one, are $10 per person.

**Is it stroller-friendly?**
The first floor and outdoor areas are passable. The second floor is stairs-only, but there's an exterior wheelchair ramp at the back of the building (the old Turntable Pit) that staff can point you to.

**Is there a place to nurse or change a baby?**
Restrooms are small and historic; assume there's no changing table and pack a portable pad. There is no dedicated nursing room.

**Can I bring snacks?**
Best eaten outside the buildings. There's no on-site cafe or vending, and Main Street is a five-minute walk for real food.

**Are there active trains running by?**
Yes — the CSX freight line runs right behind the station and trains pass through unannounced. Train-loving kids consider this the best part. Sensory-sensitive kids should be warned the horns are loud and unpredictable.

**Is it good in the rain?**
The main building is indoor, but the caboose and the HO-scale freight house are outdoor structures you walk to. Light rain is fine; steady rain shrinks the visit to the small station building only.

**Is the Holiday Train Garden worth a separate trip?**
Yes — it's the best version of this museum. It runs late November through early January, free, and the volunteer-run layouts inside the station add a lot to the everyday experience. Expect parking to fill earlier on December weekends.
