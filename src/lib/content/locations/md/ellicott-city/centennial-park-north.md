## Overview

Centennial Park North is the entrance to go to for the playground, not the lake. It's home to Howard County's flagship all-access playground — a fully fenced, poured-rubber play area built so a kid in a wheelchair and a kid on foot can play side by side, plus a sensory trail loop with music panels, texture stations, and inclusive swings. If you have a child with mobility, sensory, or communication needs, this is the one park in the area designed around them from the ground up.

It's also the North face of the bigger 337-acre Centennial Park, so you get a slice of the 2.6-mile paved lake loop, an accessible fishing pier and pond boardwalk, tennis and racquetball courts, a ball field, a fitness lot, and a skate area — all free. Skip North if what you actually want is boat rentals: the Adventure Shack sits across the lake at the South Area, and you'd walk the loop or drive around to reach it. And note the playground itself is compact — great for under-8s and inclusive play, less of a marathon for a pack of big kids.

## What to know before you go

- **Hours:** Open daily, sunrise to sunset. The skate area is unsupervised and open dawn to dusk, weather permitting.
- **Cost:** Free to enter, free to park. Nothing here costs money unless you drive over to the South Area for a boat.
- **This is the North entrance:** 9801 Old Annapolis Rd. Centennial Park has four separate gated areas (North, South, East, West); the all-access playground and sensory trail are only at North.
- **Parking:** Free dedicated lot right in front of the playground. It fills on warm weekends — the playground is small enough that a full lot means a busy playground.
- **The playground:** Roughly 4,800 sq ft, fully fenced with two gated entrances, on spongy poured-rubber surfacing that rolls for wheelchairs and strollers. A toddler structure (little train, small slide, driving wheel) and a bigger ramped structure (slides, climbers, monkey bars, wall games, spinners, mushroom steps, a music area).
- **Inclusive equipment:** Expression swings, a Zero-G swing chair, a sway glider, a we-go rocking "boat," two merry-go-rounds, a sensory rock abacus, musical stations, and a non-verbal communication (AAC) board. There are shade structures over parts of it.
- **Sensory trail:** A short loop beside the playground with music features, sensory panels, and sensory play equipment (opened November 2023).
- **Restrooms:** A permanent restroom building is by the parking lot — a short walk from the playground.
- **Also at North:** 2 tennis courts (ADA-accessible), 6 racquetball courts, a ball field, a fitness lot with cardio and strength equipment, a skate area, picnic tables, and benches.
- **Lake access:** North sits on the paved loop, with an accessible fishing pier and a boardwalk overlooking the pond. No boat rentals here — those are at the South Area.
- **Food:** Nothing on-site at North. Pack water and snacks; there are picnic tables.
- **Dogs:** Leashed dogs are fine on the trails but not inside the playground.
- **What to bring:** Sunscreen and hats (shade is partial), water, snacks, and a change of clothes for the swings and slides.

## Tips for families

- **This is the accessibility play destination, so plan for company.** Because it's the flagship all-access playground, families drive in from across the county — weekend late mornings and early afternoons are the crush. A weekday morning or a right-after-breakfast weekend start gets you a near-empty playground.
- **The full fence is the toddler win.** Two gated entrances and a complete perimeter mean a runner can't bolt for the parking lot or the pond. It's one of the few playgrounds here where you can sit on a bench and breathe.
- **Swings are inclusive-style, not bucket swings.** You'll find expression swings and a Zero-G chair rather than the usual row of infant bucket seats. Great for kids who need support, but if your baby only tolerates a standard bucket swing, know that going in.
- **The rubber surface bakes in summer sun.** Poured rubber gets hot by midday in July and August. Come early, aim for the shaded structures, and check equipment temperature before a bare-legged toddler slides down.
- **Pair the playground with the loop.** Walk or stroll a stretch of the paved lake path from the North lot for a change of pace, then circle back — the accessible pier and boardwalk are a good "watch the ducks" reset when the playground gets loud.
- **Bring the racquets or the board.** Two tennis and six racquetball courts and a skate area sit steps from the playground, so an older sibling doesn't have to suffer through toddler time.
- **For boats, budget a drive.** The Adventure Shack boat rentals are at the South Area (10000 Clarksville Pike), across the lake. Don't promise the kids a paddle boat from the North lot without planning the trip around.
- **Combine it with Clarks Elioak Farm.** The storybook Enchanted Forest farm is minutes up Clarksville Pike — burn energy at the playground, then slow down at the farm for an easy half-day.

## Best time to visit

- **Time of day:** Early morning is calmest and coolest. Late afternoon works once summer sun comes off the rubber surface. Midday on a warm weekend is peak crowd.
- **Day of week:** Weekday mornings are quietest by far. Weekends stay busy because this is _the_ accessible playground families seek out.
- **Season:** Spring through fall is the sweet spot. Shade structures make summer workable if you come early; fall is ideal. The playground stays open in winter, but expect cold metal, and restrooms are limited to the South Area in the off-season.
- **Weather:** Fully outdoor with no indoor backup — skip it in steady rain. Hot days need an early start and sun protection; a bundled-up cold-day visit is fine.

## FAQs

**Where do I park for the all-access playground?**
Use the North Area entrance at 9801 Old Annapolis Rd. There's a free dedicated lot directly in front of the playground. It fills up on warm-weekend afternoons, so come earlier if you can.

**Is the playground good for a child who uses a wheelchair?**
Yes. This is Howard County's flagship inclusive playground: poured-rubber surfacing that rolls for wheelchairs, ramped access to the main structure, a Zero-G swing chair, a sway glider, and other equipment you can ride without transferring out of a chair. There's ADA access to the nearby tennis courts and an accessible fishing pier too.

**Is the playground fenced?**
Yes — it's fully fenced with two gated entrances. It's one of the few playgrounds in the area where a toddler can't slip out toward the parking lot or the water.

**How long should we plan for?**
Plan 60–90 minutes for the playground and sensory trail. Add time if you'll walk part of the lake loop, hit the courts, or use the skate area, and it easily becomes a morning.

**Are there bathrooms?**
Yes — a permanent restroom building sits by the parking lot, a short walk from the playground. In the colder months, the South Area is the restroom that stays open.

**Can we rent boats or paddle boats here?**
Not at North. The Adventure Shack boat rentals are at the South Area (10000 Clarksville Pike) on the other side of the lake. You can reach it on the paved loop or by driving around.

**Is it stroller-friendly?**
Yes. The rubber surfacing and the paved lake path both roll easily, so strollers and wagons are no problem.

**Are dogs allowed?**
Leashed dogs are welcome on the trails but not inside the fenced playground.
