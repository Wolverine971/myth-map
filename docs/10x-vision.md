# Tiny Tribe Adventures — The 10x Vision

> Companion to [promotion-strategy.md](./promotion-strategy.md) (the formal plan) and [promotion-scrappy.md](./promotion-scrappy.md) (right now). This doc answers two different questions:
>
> 1. **What does 10x promotion look like — for ~2x the effort?**
> 2. **If the best person in the world built this with unlimited time and perfect taste — what does the user _feel_?**
>
> Neither is a to-do list. Both are a north star.

## Part 1 — The 10x promotion play

### The reframe

10x is not "send 10x more emails." 10x is **a different mechanism.** The scrappy plan (texting friends, posting in groups) is linear: every parent reached costs one unit of effort. The formal plan (partnerships, press) is slightly better but still linear: every venue or journalist is a 1:1 negotiation.

The 10x mechanism is **stop pitching, start being the source.** Build artifacts that distribute themselves and don't decay. The work compounds instead of resetting every Monday.

There is one move that does most of this work. Then three supporting moves that each multiply it.

### The one move: a Thursday email

> **"This Weekend in Maryland with Kids."** Three picks, every Thursday afternoon. From DJ. Honest. Useful. Forwardable.

Why this is the move:

- **Email is the only channel that compounds for local content.** Social posts decay in 6 hours. Blog posts attract Google but rarely Saturday plans. Email lands at the moment of decision — Thursday is when parents start thinking about the weekend.
- **It is forwardable.** "Hey did you see this week's TTA?" → one parent forwards it, you got a new subscriber without spending a unit of effort.
- **It is repeat engagement.** A subscriber is not a one-time visit. They open it 30+ times a year. Each open is a moment to remember the site exists.
- **It is a forcing function for the product.** Writing the email every week forces you to actually visit places, take real photos, notice the things that matter to a parent. The product gets better because the email demands it.
- **It is content that gets indexed.** Each week's edition becomes an evergreen blog post on the site. In 12 months you have 50 deeply-local landing pages.

What it costs: ~2 hours a week. One pick is usually a place you've already been with your kids. One is a place you've been meaning to try. One is a "if you only do one thing this weekend" recommendation tied to weather or a seasonal event.

What it returns, realistically, in 12 months at honest growth rates: 500–2,000 subscribers, of whom 30–40% open it most weeks. Compounding referral traffic from the indexed back catalog. A real audience that you don't have to re-earn every week.

### The three things that multiply it

**1. Be the LLM's source, not its target.**

Parents now ask ChatGPT and Perplexity "what should we do with our 3-year-old in Baltimore this weekend?" Right now the AI gives them a generic, slightly wrong list. The 10x version is: when the AI answers that question, **it cites tinytribeadventures.com.** That's a position, not a campaign. You get there by:

- Publishing weekly content with real specifics LLMs can quote (parking, ages, hours, the kind of practical detail Yelp doesn't have).
- Structured data on every location page so crawlers and AI tooling parse it cleanly.
- Topic pages that match how parents actually search: "indoor with toddler when it's raining" beats "kid-friendly Baltimore restaurants."

This is durable distribution. Once you're in the model's training and retrieval set, the traffic doesn't stop on Monday.

**2. Show up where parents already are, in atoms not bits.**

A 4×6 postcard with the map's URL and a QR code, in the dish at the museum gift shop. A magnet on a stack at the library checkout. A sticker handed out at the school's spring fair. Each one is a billboard that costs $0.40 and lasts months. Venues say yes to these because they're useful to their visitors — no partnership contract needed, just a polite ask.

You ask 30 venues to put a stack of cards by their register. 10 say yes. 200 parents pick one up over a season. 40 visit the site. 10 sign up for the email. That's a slow, real, never-decaying funnel built from one Saturday at Kinko's.

**3. Let parents add the places.**

Right now you add every location. That doesn't scale and it leaves the best signal on the table — _what your readers actually love._ A 30-second submission form ("we tried this last weekend, here's what works, here's what doesn't, photo optional") turns every visitor into a potential contributor. The first 10 submissions you'll add by hand. By submission 100, half of them go up with light editing. Each contributor tells their friends "I'm on this map." That's the loop.

### What 2x effort looks like, allocated

A normal week, ~6 hours total on Tiny Tribe Adventures:

- **2 hours**: write Thursday's email, including visiting (or revisiting) a place with the kids.
- **1 hour**: turn last week's email into a polished evergreen post on the site.
- **1 hour**: respond to 1–2 reader emails, approve 1–2 submitted locations.
- **1 hour**: one in-person drop (postcards at the library; cards to a venue) OR one structured-data improvement to a location page.
- **1 hour**: slack — for the part of the project that should feel like a hobby, because if it doesn't, you'll quit.

Six hours a week, compounded for a year, is a different project. Not a side project anymore — a real local institution with 2,000 readers, 200 indexed pages, and venues that _ask_ to be on it.

## Part 2 — What it feels like, when it's perfect

> Forget infrastructure, performance, frameworks. Just describe the parent's day.

### Friday, 9:14pm

The kids are finally asleep. The dishwasher is running. There's that low, familiar feeling — _we should do something tomorrow._ Not a vacation. Just a Saturday. The dread is not about the kids. The dread is about the next 15 minutes of Google rabbit hole that's going to end with "let's just go to the park again."

She opens her phone. She has tinytribeadventures.com bookmarked because her friend texted it to her last month.

### The first second

The site already knows it's Saturday tomorrow. It already knows she's in Towson. The first thing she sees isn't a logo or a sign-up — it's **three real options for tomorrow**, with the weather already baked in. "Rain expected after 2pm" is right there. The first card is an indoor place she's never heard of, 14 minutes away. The second is an outdoor thing that's good in the morning if they leave by 10. The third is a backup if it pours.

She didn't ask for this. The site offered it.

The feeling is **someone already thought about this for me.** That feeling, in that first second, is the whole product.

### Tapping in

She taps the first card. The location page opens. There is no carousel. There is no booking widget. There is no "5.0 stars from 247 reviews."

There is:

- One photo. A real photo. A blurry, honest photo that looks like another parent took it on a Tuesday. The kid in the corner is half-visible and a little bored. She immediately trusts it more than any glamour shot.
- Two short paragraphs, written like a friend texting: _"We took our 3-year-old here in February. Bring snacks — the cafe is overpriced. The play area in the back is the best part. Skip the upstairs unless your kid is over 6."_
- A small, plain row of facts: indoor, ages 0–7, free, parking out front, 14 minutes from you.
- A "good for" tag: _rainy mornings_. Not a category. A use case.
- At the bottom, three other places within 10 minutes — because she's not planning a single stop, she's planning a day.

She taps "add to Saturday." The site builds a plan: this place at 10, a casual lunch nearby at noon, a backup playground if there's time. She didn't ask for an itinerary. The site offered one.

She texts the plan to her husband. One tap. He gets a link that opens the same plan on his phone.

### Saturday, 9:53am

They pull into the parking lot. She opens the site again. The page knows they've arrived — it shows the "first 10 minutes" tip: _"Bathrooms are by the gift shop, not the entrance. Stroller fits through the side door. Don't pay for the upstairs."_

This is the part no other site has. This is the part written by a parent who actually went. It's worth more than every glossy photo on every competing site combined.

### After

Tuesday morning. She gets a single, gentle email. Not a survey. Not a newsletter. Just: _"How was Saturday?"_ Three buttons. She taps "loved it." That's it. Her one tap becomes a sentence on the location page for the next parent: _"Other parents loved this place in May."_

She doesn't think about that. She just feels heard.

### The voice

The site never shouts. There is no "JOIN NOW." There is no popup. There is no email gate. There is no cookies banner the size of a couch.

There is no marketing on the site. There is no "About Us" page that talks about a "mission." The about page is one paragraph long and ends with "this is Zach, Kerry, DJ, and Anna."

When the site disagrees with a place, it says so out loud. _"This museum is great for kids 6+. We tried it with a 2-year-old and regretted it. Wait a year."_ Honesty is the whole moat. Every other site is selling something. This one isn't. Parents can feel that within 30 seconds.

### The repeat

Three weeks later she catches herself opening the site without thinking. The dread on Friday night is shorter now. She doesn't even think of it as "going to a website" anymore. It's the thing she checks when it's Friday, the way she checks the weather when she's about to walk out.

She has never made an account. She has never been asked to make one. Her saves are still there. Her family's plan from last weekend is still there. The site has never asked her to remember a password, install an app, or accept a notification.

She has texted the site to four friends. She didn't notice doing it.

### The arc

What the site replaces is not a competing app. **It replaces the 15 minutes of dread on Friday night.** That's the job. Every design decision has to defend that job. Every feature has to either shorten those 15 minutes, make the resulting plan better, or stay out of the way.

The single sentence the brand never says out loud, but earns: _You're not figuring this out alone._

## Part 3 — What this changes about right now

This vision is not the next 30 days. It's a north star. But it does change two things about what to do this week:

1. **Start the Thursday email this Thursday.** Not "build a newsletter system" — literally write three picks in a note, send it to 5 friends. Tomorrow's version is Buttondown or Resend, $0/month, an email signup on the homepage. The 10x play has a single starting move and this is it.

2. **Rewrite three location pages in the new voice.** Not a redesign. Just rewrite the copy on three pages to sound like a parent texting another parent: real photo, what worked, what didn't, the "first 10 minutes" tip. Use these as the proof-of-feel before redoing the rest. If the new voice doesn't ship on three pages this month, the rest of the vision is just words.

Everything else in this doc can wait. These two cannot, because they are the cheapest possible test of whether the 10x version is real or just a nice story.
