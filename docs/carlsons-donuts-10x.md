# Carlson's Donuts — 10x Vision

A brainstorm for what Carlson's Donuts (Severn, MD — family-run cake-donut counter, in business since 1981) could become if amplified rather than modernized. The frame: take what's already mythic about it and make it more visible, more legible, more collectible — without scaling, franchising, or digitizing.

## What it should feel like to visit

You pull into the lot in the dark. There are already eight cars. Inside, the case is fuller than you've ever seen it because you got here at 5:42 a.m. for the first time in your life, and the woman behind the counter says "first time?" without it being weird. There is a leather book on the counter the size of a Bible. You sign page 11,847. Your kid signs underneath you in crayon.

Behind the counter, the back wall is **covered in Polaroids**, four decades deep, in unsorted chronological drifts. Donuts. Babies. A guy in 1988 holding up the biggest chop suey ever made. You realize, with a small jolt, that someone's grandfather is up there.

Today's case has **fourteen chop sueys**. Each one has a tiny number stuck to the front of its tray. The kid points at #6 because it looks like a horseshoe. The man working the case takes a Polaroid of #6, hands it to your kid with the box, and writes "5/13 — Henry, age 4" on the white margin in pencil. That Polaroid will be on the refrigerator for a year. It will eventually go in a shoebox. Your kid will find it when he's 22.

Tucked under the box lid is a single sheet of typewriter paper: **today's Carlson's Almanac.** Front: weather, the number of consecutive days they've fried donuts (14,234), a one-paragraph story about a regular who turned 80 this week. Back: a hand-drawn map showing the BWI plane-watching spot six minutes east. Free with every order. There are 14,234 of these in the world now, and people collect them.

You leave at 5:51 a.m. with a pink box, a Polaroid, an Almanac, and the distinct sensation that you just visited something — not bought something.

**The emotional target:** a visitor leaves with the feeling that they just touched something rare on purpose. Not "I got donuts." Not "the line was worth it." But: _I was there this morning, on this specific day, and I have a Polaroid that proves it, and nobody in the world has the same one._

## The five moves — 10x value for 2x effort

### 1. Make the chop suey the hero, not the oddity

Right now the chop suey is the secret menu item that sells out at 9 a.m. Number them. Photograph each one with a Polaroid before it goes in the case. When a customer buys #6, they get the Polaroid.

- **Cost:** a $90 Fujifilm Instax, two packs of film a day, one extra minute per batch.
- **Result:** every customer leaves with a physical, unrepeatable artifact of _the specific donut they ate today._
- **Why it works:** no chain in the country can copy this because the whole point is that each one is different. The chop suey was already the genius product — this makes the genius legible.

### 2. The book

A single hand-bound ledger on the counter. First-timers sign it. Date, name, who they brought. That's it.

- It costs nothing and becomes the most important object in the store within five years.
- New customers flip through it and find their dad's name from 1994.
- The book is the antidote to "we have an app now." Don't digitize it. Don't photograph it. The fact that it exists in one physical location is the _entire point._

### 3. The Almanac

One sheet of paper, typed on an actual typewriter, photocopied, free with every order. Hyper-local, daily, weird.

- Today's weather. Yesterday's chop suey count. A regular's birthday. A short note about why the apple fritters were lighter today (humidity).
- **Why it works:** it's a piece of media that exists _only here_, weighs nothing, costs pennies, and gives every visit a reason to be different from every other visit. After a year you have 300 issues and a low-key cult.

### 4. Bake-in the pairing

Carlson's is already a 10-minute stop. Lean all the way in: every box has a different "next stop" printed on the inside of the lid each month — the aircraft observation area, the B&A Trail, a specific playground, a quiet stretch of the Severn.

- You become the start of a _route_, not a destination.
- The donuts get better because they're eaten in a specific place.
- Other small businesses on the route start to send people back. You've built a regional ritual without scaling anything.

### 5. The 4:30 a.m. apprentice slot

One Saturday a month, one kid (8–14) gets to come in the back at 4:30 a.m., wear an apron, and help with one batch. Booked a year in advance. Capped at twelve kids a year.

- The waitlist becomes legendary because the supply is real.
- Parents will plan vacations around it.
- The kids will tell that story for the rest of their lives.
- **Effort:** one extra hour, twelve times a year. **Output:** a generation of evangelists who will never let this place close.

## What is NOT on the list

- A website
- Online ordering
- An Instagram strategy
- A second location
- A wholesale program
- A coffee program
- A branded merch line
- A loyalty app

Every one of those would _subtract_ value. Carlson's competitive moat is everything a venture-backed donut startup can't reproduce: scarcity, irregularity, analog warmth, a single physical location, and 45 years of compounded ritual. The 10x version doesn't add modern infrastructure. It makes the existing weirdness more visible, more legible, and more collectible.

## The best-entrepreneur-in-the-world version

They wouldn't grow Carlson's into a chain. They would grow it into a **landmark**. The frame shifts from "small donut shop trying to survive" to "one of the last great pre-industrial food rituals on the Eastern Seaboard, and you can drive there." That framing changes everything downstream — pricing, press, the kind of customer who shows up, how the family talks about the work to themselves.

Three quiet long-game moves:

### The 50-year book

In 2031, Carlson's hits 50. Start collecting now: every regular's name, every story, every Polaroid. Hand-typeset, hand-bound, sold only at the counter, only in cash, only on the anniversary day. One print run. People will fly in.

### The apprenticeship as succession

The single biggest risk to Carlson's is what happens when the current family stops frying. Quietly identify one young person in the community — not a hire, a _successor_ — and start the multi-year hand-off. The story of "the kid who showed up at 4:30 a.m. every Saturday for seven years" is the only acceptable next chapter.

### The deliberate refusal

Publish (on paper, in the Almanac) the things Carlson's will never do: never franchise, never ship, never open a second location, never sell online, never take a phone order under a dozen. The constraints become the brand. People who love Carlson's love that it refuses things. Make the refusal explicit and it becomes a feature, not an absence.
